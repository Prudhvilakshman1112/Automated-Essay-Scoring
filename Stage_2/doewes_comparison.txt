KEY FINDINGS FROM DOEWES WE ARE USING
---------------------------------------------------------
1. PERMUTATION / WORD-SALAD TEST (Chapter 4)
   Doewes shuffled words in essays. Models still gave
   high scores to meaningless "word salad" as long as
   the essay was long. This is the core of the
   "statistical shortcut" theory.

   → Our Fluff Attack directly tests the same idea:
     does adding meaningless repetitive filler inflate
     the score? This is our version of the word-salad
     test, applied to the Nie (2025) LSTM model.

2. LENGTH-SCORE CORRELATION (Chapter 4)
   Doewes finds r > 0.70 between token count and final
   score in BERT-based models. He calls this a
   "statistical shortcut" — the model is NOT reading,
   it is just rewarding length.

   → Our Stage 2 computes Pearson r = 0.5876 between
     predicted score and word count. This is the same
     measurement, applied to our LSTM model.
     (Our r is slightly lower = LSTM is marginally less
     biased than BERT in this regard.)

3. QWK AS PRIMARY METRIC (Chapter 2 & 3)
   Doewes uses QWK as the standard agreement metric and
   shows it can hide reliability problems.

   → Our Stage 2 keeps QWK = 0.9486 (accuracy preserved)
     while separately showing r = 0.5876 (bias exposed).
     This is exactly the two-metric approach Doewes
     advocates: QWK proves accuracy, a second metric
     proves (un)fairness.

4. ASAP DATASET (Chapter 3)
   Doewes uses the ASAP dataset for all experiments.
   → We also use ASAP — results are directly comparable.
